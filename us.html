Key metrics explained

Click-through rate (CTR): % of delivered emails where someone clicked a link. Higher = more engaging content.
Unsubscribe rate: % of recipients who opted out after that send. Lower = healthier list.
Delivery rate: % of sent emails that actually reached inboxes (not bounced). 99%+ is excellent.
Spam rate: % of recipients who marked the email as spam. 0% is perfect.
Correlation (r): Statistical measure from -1 to +1. Close to 0 = no relationship. Close to 1 = strong positive relationship.
Open rate: % of delivered emails that were opened. Indicates subject line effectiveness and audience interest. Data available for 12 of 19 dispatches.
Article views: Daily count of readers viewing US research content. Lift on dispatch days indicates email-driven traffic.

Click-through rate over time

Click-through rate is highly volatile (2.8% to 14.6%) with no clear directional trend. The spikes at 14.6% (12 Sep) and 14.6% (9 Feb) show that certain content resonates far more than others. The 21 Nov dispatch was the worst performer at 4.1% CTR.

March 2026 shows a dramatic drop (2.8% on 2 Mar) — the lowest dispatch of the entire period. This coincides with the most recent send, suggesting either a significant content issue or timing problem in early March.

February 2026 rebounded strongly with two high-performing dispatches (14.6% on 9 Feb and 10.3% on 2 Feb) despite a weak start (6.5% on 17 Feb). Average CTR of 9.4% with moderate unsubscribes (0.27%).

Open rate vs click-through rate

Open rates range from 29% to 58% across the 12 measured dispatches, averaging 41.3%. The 17 Feb dispatch stands out at 58% open rate — the highest measured. Conversely, the latest dispatch (2 Mar) had the lowest open rate at 29%, mirroring its lowest-ever CTR (2.8%).

December and January showed depressed open rates (30–32%) suggesting seasonal disengagement or subject line fatigue during the holiday period. February bounced back strongly to 50.3% average, the best month measured.

Overall summary & recommendations

What's going well

Solid CTR (8.7%) with zero spam. The US program shows good engagement for a smaller list (~4,700). Delivery is excellent (99.8%), and zero spam maintains sender reputation. Real-Time Dispatch (7.6% CTR) is a proven secondary channel with growing list size (520 → 1,223). The program provides consistent email marketing without deliverability issues.

What needs attention

Click-through rate is highly volatile with no clear driver. November (6.9% CTR) and March (2.8% on latest) underperformed dramatically, while September and February spiked. Send time correlation is near-zero (r = -0.15), so timing isn't the lever. Content quality appears to be the dominant factor, with a 5× spread between best (14.6%) and worst (2.8%) dispatches.

Unsubscribe rate is elevated (0.27% vs EU's 0.22%) with concerning spikes. The 2 Feb dispatch triggered 0.50% unsubscribes (affecting ~23 people), and 17 Oct and 1 Dec each saw 0.40%+. This suggests periodic content-audience misalignment or list fatigue.

No meaningful dispatch-driven traffic lift to articles (+3% vs EU's +155%). Unlike the EU program, email sends don't measurably drive readers to research articles. This indicates either that links are underperforming, the audience doesn't value article content, or readers are accessing articles through other channels (organic, social, direct).

The February views spike is unexplained. Average views jumped 2.4× (from 144–155/day to 269/day), but there's no corresponding CTR lift on dispatch day (9 Feb: 14.6% CTR, 208 views). A viral article or organic discovery likely drove the traffic surge.

Priority action items

1. Investigate the CTR variability and March performance cliff. The 2.8% CTR on 2 Mar is the lowest ever — a 70% drop from February. Analyze email content, link placement, and send metrics (time, day, subject line) to pinpoint what went wrong. Establish CTR guardrails (e.g., 7% minimum).

2. Reduce unsubscribe rate to EU levels (0.22%). The 0.27% baseline is 23% higher. Target episodes like 2 Feb (0.50%) by reviewing that dispatch's content and audience segment. Test smaller sends or segmented audiences to reduce opt-outs.

3. Identify and replicate the February views surge. With 2.4× normal traffic in Feb, something worked. Was it a specific article, social promotion, organic search win, or seasonal topic? Identify the driver and build a strategy around it.

4. Test article links in the dispatch. The weak email-to-article lift (+3%) suggests links may be underperforming or not prominent. A/B test CTAs, link placement, and call-to-action text to increase click-through to articles specifically.

5. Monitor the 16 Jan anomaly. The 7,736-recipient send (56% above baseline) had 6.4% CTR, lower than average. If this represents a new segment, track its performance separately — it may skew future metrics.
